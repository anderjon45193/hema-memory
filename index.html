HOW TO MEMORIZE

記憶術ガイド — THE METHOD

This guide uses a visual image-linking system — a proven memorization technique where abstract facts become vivid, concrete pictures in your mind. No stories, no movement. Just two frozen images, stacked and touching.

01
CONVERT WORDS TO IMAGES
Every term gets turned into a concrete, visual image — the first picture that pops into your head. Abstract or medical words become images through sound-alikes: "warfarin" sounds like "war fan", so picture a Japanese battle fan. "Heparin" becomes a "hair pin". "PTT" becomes "putt-putt golf". The sillier or more vivid, the stronger the memory.
02
STACK TWO IMAGES — BASE + TOP
Each fact is encoded as two images stacked vertically. The base image sits on the bottom. The top image sits directly on top of it, physically touching the base. This point of contact is what creates the link in your memory. Think of it like building a small sculpture — one object resting on another.
03
KEEP IT STATIC — NO ACTIONS, NO STORIES
This is critical: do not animate the scene. No movement, no storylines, no one doing anything. Just two objects frozen in place, touching each other. A war fan balanced on a PT ball — not "a war fan flying toward a ball." The stillness forces your brain to encode the spatial relationship, which is far more durable than a narrative.
04
MAKE IT VIVID AND SPECIFIC
The more detailed and unusual the image, the better it sticks. Don't just think "a fan" — picture a red and gold Japanese war fan with sharp edges. Don't think "a ball" — picture a bright blue physical therapy exercise ball, slightly deflated. Texture, color, size, and absurdity all help. If it makes you smile, it'll stick.
05
RECALL = DECODE THE IMAGES
When you need the fact, picture the scene first, then decode it. You see a war fan on a PT ball → war fan = warfarin, PT ball = prothrombin time → "PT monitors warfarin." With practice, this becomes instant. The images are just scaffolding — eventually you'll know the facts directly.

BUILDING YOUR MEMORY DATABASE

記憶の宮殿 — THE MEMORY PALACE

The image pairs above are the content — but you need somewhere to store them. A memory database gives every fact a permanent home you can walk through and retrieve at will. Here's how to build one using a place you already know.

PHASE 1 Walk Your Space
Choose a place you know well — your house, apartment, a route you walk daily. Now do a mental walkthrough and pick out 5 major landmarks in a natural order. These are your support images — permanent anchors that never change. Walk the same direction every time.

These 5 objects are your anchors. You'll always walk this route in the same order — front door first, couch last.
PHASE 2 Break Each Anchor Into 5 Sub-Parts
Every support image gets divided into 5 smaller, distinct parts (maximum). Look at the object closely in your mind and identify 5 physical features you can clearly picture. These sub-parts become individual storage slots.
🪑 EXAMPLE: ENTRY TABLE → 5 SUB-PARTS
1 Table top surface
2 Corner edge
3 Table leg
4 Leg base (above wheel)
5 Wheel / foot
Do this for all 5 support images. You now have 5 anchors × 5 sub-parts = 25 storage slots.
PHASE 3 Build 5-Image Chains
Before attaching real content, you need to extend each slot with a chain. For each sub-part, create a sequence of 5 random images linked together in pairs — the same stacking method from the first guide. Pick 5 random objects and connect them one at a time.

Pair 1: A pillow sitting on top of the headphones. (6 sec)
Pair 2: A bandana draped on top of the pillow. (6 sec)
Pair 3: A credit card laying on the bandana. (6 sec)
Pair 4: A water balloon resting on the credit card. (6 sec)

Then connect the first image of this chain (headphones) to the sub-part slot. The headphones sit on top of the table top surface. Now when you mentally visit that table top, you see headphones → and the whole chain unrolls.
Each chain gives you 5 positions. Build one chain per sub-part. Only visualize two images at a time — never the whole chain at once.
PHASE 4 Attach Your Facts
Now each position in each chain becomes a hook for real content. Take a fact's image pair from this guide and connect it to the corresponding chain image — the chain image becomes the base, with your fact's image pair stacked on it. Retrieval works by walking your space, finding the sub-part, following the chain, and decoding each fact.
